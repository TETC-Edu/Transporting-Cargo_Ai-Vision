Field Reference

The field is 100×100 cm. The goal stays in one place: AprilTag 0 between two orange barrels. The robot starts facing the field. The two sports balls sit somewhere on the far half — and they move every round. That's the catch: there's no distance to measure once and write down. The robot's AI Vision Sensor finds the balls live, every run.

Bearing · which way is it?

How to think about bearing

When the camera sees a ball, it reports a bearing — how far off-center the ball is, in degrees.

A positive bearing means the ball is to the robot's right, so it turns RIGHT. A negative bearing means it's to the left, so it turns LEFT. A bearing near 0 means it's already dead ahead.

You never measure this yourself — the AI Vision Sensor hands you the number, and your code decides which way to turn.

The translation · field to code

You don't write a distance. You write "take a look, then turn the way the camera points":

Python
ball = robot.vision.get_data(SPORTS_BALL)
if ball[0].bearing > 0:
    robot.turn_for(RIGHT, ball[0].bearing)
else:
    robot.turn_for(LEFT, -ball[0].bearing)
Reads as — take a look for a ball; if it's to the right, turn right by that angle; otherwise turn left.

What the AI Vision Sensor reports

1
.exists
Yes / no. Is the object in view right now?
2
.bearing
The angle. Positive = right, negative = left, 0 = straight ahead.
3
.centerX
0 to 320 across the camera. 160 is dead center.
4
.width
How big the object looks. Bigger means the robot is closer.
Remember: turn on robot.vision.tag_detection(True) before you look for AprilTag 0. And go slow — a fast-moving robot blurs the camera so the ball flickers in and out.
